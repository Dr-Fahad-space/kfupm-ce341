# Chapter 5: Highway Design Standards

**Geometric Design and Design Controls**

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, it should be possible to:

- **Remember:** list key design elements (lane width, shoulder, cross slope, etc.).
- **Understand:** explain how design speed controls geometric design.
- **Apply:** calculate horizontal and vertical curve parameters.
- **Analyze:** evaluate design alternatives for safety and operations.

---

## 5.1 Introduction to Geometric Design

Geometric design establishes the visible dimensions of the highway, including horizontal alignment, vertical alignment, and cross-section elements. Design is based on design speed, design vehicle, and design traffic volume.

## 5.2 Design Controls

*Required Bloom level: Understand*

Design speed is the most important controlâ€”it determines minimum curve radii, sight distances, and superelevation rates. The selected design speed should reflect the character of the terrain and the expected operating speed.

## 5.3 Horizontal Alignment

*Required Bloom level: Apply*

Horizontal alignment consists of tangents (straight sections) connected by circular curves and transition (spiral) curves. Key elements include curve radius, superelevation, and side friction.

## 5.4 Vertical Alignment

*Required Bloom level: Apply*

Vertical alignment consists of grades connected by vertical curves (crest or sag). Design ensures adequate sight distance over crests and proper drainage and headlight sight distance in sags.

## 5.5 Cross-Section Elements

*Required Bloom level: Remember*

Cross-section elements include: travel lanes, shoulders, medians, side slopes, drainage features, and clear zones. Standard widths are specified in design manuals based on design speed and traffic volume.
