CHAPTER 3

Characteristics of the Driver, the Pedestrian, the Vehicle, and the Road

Human Factors, Vehicle Dynamics, and Sight Distance in Highway Design

Highway design must accommodate the full range of users—not the average, but the less capable users most at risk. This chapter covers the human, vehicle, and road characteristics that directly control geometric design and traffic-control timing: how drivers perceive and react to information, the walking speeds that govern pedestrian signals, design-vehicle selection, braking and stopping distance, and the sight-distance criteria that connect all of these to road geometry.

3.1Design Philosophy: Designing for a Range of Users

Level: Evaluate

Designing for the "average" user means roughly half the population is not accommodated. If the average reaction time is the design value, about half of all drivers react more slowly—a dangerous situation when the consequence is a collision.

Engineers therefore use percentile-based design criteria. An 85th-percentile design accommodates 85% of users and is typical for operational parameters like operating speed. A 95th-percentile design accommodates 95% and is used where the consequences of exceeding the design value are severe, such as stopping sight distance. AASHTO's design PRT of 2.5 s, for example, covers approximately 90% of drivers—deliberately above the 85th percentile because the penalty for a longer reaction time is a potential crash.

The choice of percentile is a trade-off: higher percentiles improve safety but increase cost (longer sight distances need flatter curves and more earthwork). When stakes are higher—school zones, retirement communities, complex interchanges—engineers use higher percentiles or add supplementary safety measures.

3.2Driver Human Factors

Level: Analyse

3.2.1The PIEV Model and Perception–Reaction Time

Level: Apply

Every driver action follows a four-stage sequence called the PIEV model:

  • Perception: The driver detects a stimulus (a sign, a stopped vehicle, an obstacle).
  • Identification: The driver recognises what it is and what it means.
  • Emotion (Decision): The driver decides what to do—brake, swerve, or continue.
  • Volition (Response): The driver physically executes the action (e.g., presses the brake).

The total time for all four stages is the perception–reaction time (PRT). The vehicle keeps moving at full speed throughout this interval—no braking occurs until PRT has elapsed. PRT also governs the yellow-phase duration at signalised intersections.

AASHTO recommends a design PRT of 2.5 seconds for stopping sight distance, covering approximately 90% of drivers under normal conditions. This value may be insufficient at locations with high information loads or unexpected hazards—complex interchanges, ramp terminals, or work zones—where a longer criterion (decision sight distance) should be used.

3.1 Example 3.1 — Distance Travelled During PRTA driver travelling at 105 km/h has a PRT of 2.5 s. How far does the vehicle travel before braking begins? Level: Apply

Solution:

Convert speed: \(u = 105 \times \dfrac{1000}{3600} = 29.17\) m/s

Distance during PRT:

\[d = u \times t = 29.17 \times 2.5 = \mathbf{72.9 \text{ m}}\]

The vehicle covers nearly 73 m before any braking force is applied.

3.2.2Visual Perception and Design Implications

Level: Analyse

Visual acuity is the ability to see fine details—most importantly, to read sign text at speed. It is measured on the Snellen scale: 6/6 is normal vision. A driver with 6/4 acuity (sharper) can read the same sign from 1.5× farther; a driver with 6/10 acuity (weaker) must be 40% closer. The relationship: if a sign is just readable at distance d for a 6/6 driver, it is readable at \((6/X) \times d\) for a driver with \(6/X\) acuity. Sign letter heights are set so that drivers with below-average acuity can still read and react in time.

Most people see clearly within only about 3°–5° of their direct line of sight, with fairly clear vision out to 10°–12°. This is why traffic signs must be placed within the driver's central field of view.

3.2 Example 3.2 — Sign Legibility DistanceA sign is sized so that a 6/6 driver can just read it at 90 m. At what distance can drivers with 6/4 and 6/10 acuity read it? Level: Analyse

Solution:

6/4 acuity (sharper—resolves detail from farther):

\[d = \frac{6}{4} \times 90 = 1.5 \times 90 = \mathbf{135 \text{ m}}\]

6/10 acuity (weaker—must be closer):

\[d = \frac{6}{10} \times 90 = 0.6 \times 90 = \mathbf{54 \text{ m}}\]

The weaker-eyed driver loses 36 m of reading distance. Sign designers must size letters for the less capable end of the driving population so that these drivers still have enough distance to read and respond.

Peripheral vision covers up to 160° but with poor detail. It is most important for detecting approaching vehicles at intersections. Speed and age both narrow the effective field.

Glare recovery is the time the eyes need to readjust after exposure to a bright light (e.g., oncoming headlights). Recovery from light to dark can exceed 6 seconds and worsens significantly after age 40. Countermeasures include higher luminaire mounting heights, glare shields, and vegetated medians.

3.2.3Older Road Users

Level: Understand

Drivers aged 65+ experience reduced acuity, greater glare sensitivity, slower reaction times, narrower peripheral fields, and reduced flexibility. The practical design implications are: larger sign lettering with higher retroreflectivity, longer tapers and advance warning in work zones, simpler intersection layouts, and consideration of decision sight distance and lower pedestrian walking speeds where this population is predominant.

3.3Pedestrian Characteristics

Level: Evaluate

The key pedestrian characteristic for intersection design is walking speed, which controls the pedestrian clearance interval—the time provided for pedestrians to finish crossing after the WALK signal ends.

The standard design walking speed is 1.22 m/s when elderly pedestrians (65+) make up 20% or less of the crossing population. Where elderly pedestrians exceed 20%, speeds as low as 0.88 m/s are used. The MUTCD recommends 1.07 m/s as a general-purpose value. The engineer selects the value based on the pedestrian population at each location.

Accessible pedestrian signals (APS) provide audible tones or vibrating surfaces for visually impaired pedestrians. Kerb ramps are required for wheelchair access. These affect physical intersection design but generally do not change the clearance calculation, which remains governed by walking speed.

3.3 Example 3.3 — Pedestrian Clearance IntervalA signalised intersection has a crossing distance of 18.0 m. Find the clearance interval for (a) a general population and (b) a location near a senior care facility. Level: Apply

\[t = \frac{\text{Crossing distance}}{\text{Walking speed}}\]

(a) General population (1.22 m/s):

\[t = \frac{18.0}{1.22} = 14.8 \text{ s} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{\text{Use 15 s}}\]

(b) Elderly-predominant (0.88 m/s):

\[t = \frac{18.0}{0.88} = 20.5 \text{ s} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{\text{Use 21 s}}\]

The elderly location requires 40% more clearance time, significantly affecting cycle length and capacity.

3.4Vehicle Characteristics

Level: Evaluate

3.4.1Design Vehicle Selection

Level: Evaluate

Vehicle characteristics fall into three categories: static (size, weight—measured at rest), kinematic (motion: speed, acceleration, distance), and dynamic (forces acting during motion).

AASHTO defines 19 design vehicles representing the range of vehicle sizes. The engineer selects the one that represents the critical vehicle expected to use the facility:

  • Parking lots: Passenger car (P) — minimum turning radius ≈ 7.3 m
  • Residential intersections: Single-unit truck (SU-9) — minimum turning radius ≈ 12.7 m
  • Urban intersections with bus service: City transit bus — minimum turning radius ≈ 12.8 m
  • Freeway ramps / arterials with heavy trucks: Interstate semitrailer (WB-20) — minimum turning radius ≈ 13.7 m

The critical check is the low-speed turning path: the intersection geometry (kerb returns, lane widths, medians) must accommodate the swept path of the chosen design vehicle at speeds of 16 km/h or less.

3.4.2Acceleration

Level: Apply

Constant acceleration from initial speed \(u_0\) with acceleration \(a\):

\[u = u_0 + a \cdot t \qquad\qquad x = u_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2\]

From rest: \(u = at\) and \(x = \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

In practice, available acceleration decreases as speed increases. The speed-dependent model captures this:

\[\frac{du}{dt} = \alpha - \beta u\]

where \(\alpha\) (m/s²) is the maximum acceleration at zero speed and \(\beta\) (s⁻¹) is the decay rate. The time and distance to accelerate from \(u_0\) to \(u_t\):

\[t = \frac{1}{\beta}\ln\!\left(\frac{\alpha - \beta u_0}{\alpha - \beta u_t}\right) \qquad\qquad x = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}\,t - \frac{\alpha - \beta u_0}{\beta^2}\!\left(1 - e^{-\beta t}\right)\]

3.4 Example 3.4 — Acceleration Lane LengthA vehicle's acceleration follows \(du/dt = 1.0 - 0.025u\) (m/s). Find the lane length needed to reach 88.5 km/h from rest. Level: Apply

\(\alpha = 1.0\), \(\beta = 0.025\), \(u_0 = 0\), \(u_t = 88.5 \times \frac{1000}{3600} = 24.58\) m/s

Time:

\[t = \frac{1}{0.025}\ln\!\left(\frac{1.0}{1.0 - 0.025 \times 24.58}\right) = 40\ln(2.594) = 40 \times 0.953 = 38.1 \text{ s}\]

Distance:

\[x = 40(38.1) - 1600(1 - 0.3855) = 1524 - 983 = \mathbf{541 \text{ m}}\]

3.4.3Resistance Forces

Level: Understand

Four forces resist vehicle motion: air resistance (proportional to speed squared—dominant at highway speeds), grade resistance (weight component along the slope—slows vehicles on upgrades, accelerates them on downgrades), rolling resistance (tyre–pavement deformation), and curve resistance (lateral forces on front wheels in curves).

The main practical effect: heavy trucks lose speed on upgrades and gain speed on downgrades. A typical heavy truck starting at 90 km/h on a 4% upgrade will slow to about 60 km/h within 2 km, affecting capacity, level of service, and the need for climbing lanes.

3.4.4Braking Distance and Stopping Sight Distance

Level: Apply

The braking distance from speed \(u\) (km/h) on a grade \(G\):

\[D_b = \frac{u^2}{254\!\left(\dfrac{a}{g} \pm G\right)}\]

where \(a/g\) is the deceleration as a fraction of gravity (AASHTO recommends \(a/g = 0.35\), corresponding to 3.41 m/s²), and \(G\) is the grade as a decimal. Use + when travelling uphill (gravity assists braking), − when travelling downhill (gravity opposes braking).

Stopping sight distance (SSD) adds the PRT distance to the braking distance:

\[\boxed{SSD = 0.278 \cdot u \cdot t + \frac{u^2}{254\!\left(\dfrac{a}{g} \pm G\right)}}\]

where \(u\) is in km/h and \(t\) is PRT in seconds. On level roads (\(G = 0\)): \(SSD = 0.278ut + \frac{u^2}{88.9}\).

When decelerating to a lower speed \(u_2\) rather than stopping:

\[D = \frac{u_1^2 - u_2^2}{254\!\left(\dfrac{a}{g} \pm G\right)}\]

3.5 Example 3.5 — SSD on a Level RoadCalculate the stopping sight distance for a design speed of 100 km/h on a level road. Use PRT = 2.5 s and \(a/g\) = 0.35. Level: Apply

PRT distance: \(d_1 = 0.278 \times 100 \times 2.5 = 69.5\) m

Braking distance: \(D_b = \dfrac{100^2}{254 \times 0.35} = \dfrac{10{,}000}{88.9} = 112.5\) m

\[SSD = 69.5 + 112.5 = \mathbf{182.0 \text{ m}}\]

3.6 Example 3.6 — Stopping Distance on a DowngradeA motorist at 88 km/h on a 5% downgrade sees a blocked road and stops 9 m short of the obstruction. How far was the motorist from the obstruction when first observed? Level: Analyse

On a downgrade, gravity opposes braking → use the minus sign (\(0.35 - 0.05\)):

\[SSD = 0.278 \times 88 \times 2.5 + \frac{88^2}{254(0.35 - 0.05)} = 61.2 + \frac{7744}{76.2} = 61.2 + 101.6 = 162.8 \text{ m}\]

Total distance = \(162.8 + 9 = \mathbf{171.8 \text{ m}}\)

3.7 Example 3.7 — Effect of Grade on Braking DistanceA driver needs 5.64 m more to stop on a 5% downgrade than on a 3% downgrade at the same speed. Find the speed and the braking distance when travelling uphill on the 5% grade. Level: Analyse

Set up the two downhill braking distances:

\(D_{5\%} = u^2 / 76.20\) and \(D_{3\%} = u^2 / 81.28\)

Solve for speed:

\[\frac{u^2}{76.20} - \frac{u^2}{81.28} = 5.64 \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; u^2(0.01312 - 0.01230) = 5.64 \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; u = 82.9 \text{ km/h}\]

Uphill braking on 5% grade (gravity now helps → use \(0.35 + 0.05\)):

\[D = \frac{82.9^2}{254 \times 0.40} = \frac{6872}{101.6} = \mathbf{67.6 \text{ m}}\]

Compared to the 5% downgrade distance of \(6872/76.2 = 90.2\) m, the uphill braking distance is 25% shorter—grade has a substantial effect.

3.8 Example 3.8 — Exit-Ramp DecelerationA motorist at 105 km/h must slow to 56 km/h on a ramp with a 3% downgrade. What braking distance is needed? Level: Apply

\[D = \frac{105^2 - 56^2}{254(0.35 - 0.03)} = \frac{11{,}025 - 3136}{81.28} = \frac{7889}{81.28} = \mathbf{97.1 \text{ m}}\]

3.5Road Characteristics: Sight Distance

Level: Analyse

3.5.1Stopping Sight Distance (SSD)

Level: Apply

Stopping sight distance is the minimum distance a driver needs to see an object, react, and stop. It must be provided at every point on every highway. The SSD equation combines PRT distance and braking distance:

\[SSD = 0.278 \cdot u \cdot t + \frac{u^2}{254\!\left(\dfrac{a}{g} \pm G\right)}\]

SSD requirements dictate minimum vertical-curve lengths and minimum horizontal-curve radii. On upgrades, braking distances are shorter; on downgrades, longer. The downgrade condition always produces the controlling (longest) SSD and must always be checked.

3.9 Example 3.9 — SSD on Upgrade vs. DowngradeA highway has a design speed of 110 km/h. Compute the SSD for (a) a 4% upgrade and (b) a 4% downgrade. Use PRT = 2.5 s and \(a/g = 0.35\). Which condition governs? Level: Analyse

PRT distance (same for both): \(d_1 = 0.278 \times 110 \times 2.5 = 76.5\) m

(a) 4% upgrade (\(0.35 + 0.04 = 0.39\)):

\[D_b = \frac{110^2}{254 \times 0.39} = \frac{12{,}100}{99.1} = 122.1 \text{ m} \qquad SSD = 76.5 + 122.1 = \mathbf{198.6 \text{ m}}\]

(b) 4% downgrade (\(0.35 - 0.04 = 0.31\)):

\[D_b = \frac{110^2}{254 \times 0.31} = \frac{12{,}100}{78.7} = 153.7 \text{ m} \qquad SSD = 76.5 + 153.7 = \mathbf{230.2 \text{ m}}\]

The downgrade SSD is 31.6 m longer. The downgrade governs design.

3.5.2Other Sight Distance Types

Level: Understand

Decision sight distance (DSD) applies where drivers face unexpected or complex decisions—interchange ramp terminals, lane drops, work zones. DSD uses longer PRT assumptions and produces values much larger than SSD. At 80 km/h, DSD can exceed 270 m compared to about 128 m for SSD.

Passing sight distance (PSD) applies to two-lane, two-way highways where drivers must see far enough to safely complete a passing manoeuvre. At 100 km/h, PSD is approximately 240 m.

3.6Horizontal Curve Design

Level: Evaluate

When a vehicle travels around a horizontal curve, the required centripetal force is provided by a combination of the road's superelevation (\(e\), the transverse banking toward the curve centre) and the side-friction factor (\(f_s\), the lateral tyre–pavement friction). The minimum-radius equation is:

\[\boxed{R = \frac{u^2}{127(e + f_s)}}\]

where \(R\) is the minimum radius (m) and \(u\) is design speed (km/h).

Maximum superelevation (\(e_{\max}\)): AASHTO recommends 0.10 for rural highways without snow/ice; 0.08–0.10 in snow/ice areas; 0.08 for urban expressways. Local urban roads are typically not superelevated.

Side-friction factor decreases with speed (drivers tolerate less lateral force at high speeds, and tyre–pavement friction decreases):

Design Speed (km/h) 50607080 90100110120
Side-friction factor \(f_s\) 0.190.170.150.14 0.130.120.110.09
3.10 Example 3.10 — Minimum Curve RadiusDesign speed = 100 km/h, \(e_{\max}\) = 0.08. Find the minimum radius. Level: Apply

From the table: \(f_s = 0.12\) at 100 km/h.

\[R = \frac{100^2}{127(0.08 + 0.12)} = \frac{10{,}000}{25.4} = \mathbf{394 \text{ m}}\]

3.11 Example 3.11 — Evaluating a Constrained RadiusRadius = 120 m, Design speed = 80 km/h. Is this design feasible? Level: Evaluate

From the table: \(f_s = 0.14\) at 80 km/h.

\[e + f_s = \frac{u^2}{127R} = \frac{6400}{15{,}240} = 0.420 \quad\longrightarrow\quad e = 0.420 - 0.14 = 0.280\]

A superelevation of 0.28 far exceeds the AASHTO maximum of 0.08–0.10. This design is not feasible. The engineer must either increase the radius, reduce the design speed, or treat the curve as a hazardous location with advisory speed signs and enhanced delineation.

This illustrates a critical principle: always check that the required superelevation falls within practical limits. The equation is necessary but not sufficient—engineering judgement is essential.

End-of-Chapter Problems

Conceptual Questions (Problems 1–3) and Computational Problems (Problems 4–12). Bloom's taxonomy level is indicated for each problem.

1
A colleague argues that designing for the 50th-percentile (average) driver is sufficient because "most drivers are close to average." Explain why this reasoning is flawed and what percentile range is typically used in highway design.
Level: Understand
Solution:

The 50th percentile means half the driving population performs worse than the design value. For reaction time, this means roughly half of all drivers would need more time to respond than the design provides, potentially leading to crashes. Highway design typically uses the 85th to 95th percentile: the 85th for operational values like operating speed, and the 95th for safety-critical parameters like stopping sight distance. The recommended PRT of 2.5 s covers about 90% of drivers. Higher percentiles are warranted when consequences are severe (crash, fatality) or when the population includes a high proportion of less capable users (elderly, impaired).

2
A city is redesigning intersections near a large retirement community. List three specific design adjustments the engineer should consider and explain the human-factors basis for each.
Level: Analyse
Solution:
  1. Use larger sign lettering with higher retroreflectivity. Basis: Older drivers have reduced visual acuity and reduced night vision. Larger, more reflective signs compensate and ensure readability at the required distance.
  2. Use a lower pedestrian walking speed (0.88 m/s instead of 1.22 m/s) for signal timing. Basis: Elderly pedestrians walk more slowly. The standard speed would not provide enough clearance time for them to cross safely.
  3. Simplify intersection layouts and consider decision sight distance instead of standard SSD. Basis: Older drivers have slower reaction times and process complex information more slowly. Simpler layouts and longer sight distances give them more time to perceive, decide, and respond.
3
Explain why the downgrade condition always produces the controlling (longest) stopping sight distance, even though the PRT distance is the same for both upgrade and downgrade at the same speed.
Level: Analyse
Solution:

The PRT distance (\(0.278 \times u \times t\)) depends only on speed and PRT—it is identical for upgrades and downgrades. The difference is in the braking distance: \(D_b = u^2 / [254(a/g \pm G)]\). On an upgrade, gravity assists braking, making the effective deceleration \((a/g + G)\)—larger denominator, shorter braking distance. On a downgrade, gravity opposes braking, giving \((a/g - G)\)—smaller denominator, longer braking distance. Since the braking distance is always longer on a downgrade, the total SSD is always longer. The designer must provide at least the downgrade SSD.

4
A driver travelling at 90 km/h has a PRT of 2.5 s. Calculate the distance the vehicle travels before braking begins.
Level: Apply
Solution:

\(u = 90 \times \dfrac{1000}{3600} = 25.0\) m/s

\[d = 25.0 \times 2.5 = \mathbf{62.5 \text{ m}}\]

5
Calculate the SSD for a design speed of 80 km/h on a level road. Use PRT = 2.5 s and \(a/g = 0.35\).
Level: Apply
Solution:

PRT distance: \(d_1 = 0.278 \times 80 \times 2.5 = 55.6\) m

Braking distance: \(D_b = \dfrac{80^2}{254 \times 0.35} = \dfrac{6400}{88.9} = 72.0\) m

\[SSD = 55.6 + 72.0 = \mathbf{127.6 \text{ m}}\]

6
A motorist is travelling at 100 km/h on a 6% downgrade. Calculate the SSD. Then recalculate for a 6% upgrade at the same speed. What is the difference, and what does it tell you about the importance of grade?
Level: Analyse
Solution:

PRT distance (both): \(d_1 = 0.278 \times 100 \times 2.5 = 69.5\) m

6% downgrade (\(0.35 - 0.06 = 0.29\)):

\(D_b = 10{,}000 / (254 \times 0.29) = 135.8\) m → \(SSD = 69.5 + 135.8 = \mathbf{205.3 \text{ m}}\)

6% upgrade (\(0.35 + 0.06 = 0.41\)):

\(D_b = 10{,}000 / (254 \times 0.41) = 96.0\) m → \(SSD = 69.5 + 96.0 = \mathbf{165.5 \text{ m}}\)

Difference: 39.8 m (downgrade SSD is ~24% longer). At steep grades, grade effects are substantial and the downgrade governs design.

7
A signalised intersection has a crossing distance of 24.0 m. It is adjacent to a rehabilitation hospital, and approximately 35% of pedestrians are elderly or mobility-impaired. Determine the required pedestrian clearance interval and justify your choice of walking speed.
Level: Evaluate
Solution:

With 35% elderly/impaired pedestrians—well above the 20% threshold—the lower design walking speed of 0.88 m/s must be used.

\[t = \frac{24.0}{0.88} = 27.3 \text{ s} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{\text{Use 28 s}}\]

Using the standard 1.22 m/s would give only \(24.0/1.22 = 19.7\) s ≈ 20 s, leaving a substantial portion of the crossing population unable to complete the crossing safely.

8
A vehicle's acceleration is described by \(du/dt = 1.2 - 0.030u\) (m/s units). Calculate the distance required to accelerate from rest to 72 km/h.
Level: Apply
Solution:

\(\alpha = 1.2\), \(\beta = 0.030\), \(u_0 = 0\), \(u_t = 72 \times \frac{1000}{3600} = 20.0\) m/s

Time:

\[t = \frac{1}{0.030}\ln\!\left(\frac{1.2}{1.2 - 0.030 \times 20.0}\right) = 33.33\ln\!\left(\frac{1.2}{0.6}\right) = 33.33 \times 0.6931 = 23.1 \text{ s}\]

Distance:

\[x = \frac{1.2}{0.030}(23.1) - \frac{1.2}{0.0009}(1 - e^{-0.693}) = 40(23.1) - 1333.3(1 - 0.500)\]

\[x = 924.0 - 666.7 = \mathbf{257 \text{ m}}\]

9
Determine the minimum radius for a horizontal curve on a highway with a design speed of 90 km/h and \(e_{\max} = 0.08\).
Level: Apply
Solution:

\(f_s = 0.13\) at 90 km/h (from table).

\[R = \frac{90^2}{127(0.08 + 0.13)} = \frac{8100}{26.67} = \mathbf{303.7 \text{ m}}\]

10
A two-lane rural highway (design speed 70 km/h) passes through a mountainous area with heavy snowfall. At one location, the curve radius is limited to 80 m. Evaluate whether this curve is feasible and recommend what the engineer should do.
Level: Evaluate
Solution:

\(f_s = 0.15\) at 70 km/h. Heavy snowfall → use \(e_{\max} = 0.08\).

Required superelevation:

\[e + f_s = \frac{70^2}{127 \times 80} = \frac{4900}{10{,}160} = 0.482 \quad\longrightarrow\quad e = 0.482 - 0.15 = 0.332\]

A superelevation of 0.33 is more than four times the 0.08 maximum. Not feasible at 70 km/h.

Minimum feasible radius at this speed: \(R_{\min} = 4900 / [127 \times 0.23] = 167.7\) m — but only 80 m is available.

Maximum safe speed at \(R = 80\) m:

\[u = \sqrt{127 \times 80 \times (0.08 + 0.15)} = \sqrt{2336.8} = 48.3 \text{ km/h}\]

Recommendation: Post an advisory speed of 45–50 km/h with advance warning signs, and add safety treatments (chevrons, reflective markers, rumble strips, maximum superelevation). Alternatively, realign the road to increase the radius if the budget permits.

11
A motorist at 95 km/h on an expressway approaches an exit ramp with a maximum speed of 50 km/h. The ramp has a 4% upgrade. At what distance before the ramp entrance must the driver begin braking?
Level: Analyse
Solution:

Decelerate from \(u_1 = 95\) to \(u_2 = 50\) km/h on a 4% upgrade (gravity assists → use \(+\)):

\[D = \frac{95^2 - 50^2}{254(0.35 + 0.04)} = \frac{9025 - 2500}{99.06} = \frac{6525}{99.06} = \mathbf{65.9 \text{ m}}\]

If the ramp were on a 4% downgrade instead: \(D = 6525 / [254 \times 0.31] = 82.9\) m — about 26% longer, showing how grade affects braking.

12
You are reviewing a design for a new urban arterial. The design uses a PRT of 2.5 s for all SSD calculations, including a section through a complex interchange with lane drops and merging traffic. A colleague says the standard PRT is adequate because "AASHTO recommends 2.5 s." Do you agree? Explain.
Level: Evaluate
Solution:

No. The 2.5 s PRT is the standard for stopping sight distance, which assumes an expected stimulus (a straightforward "stop for an obstacle" scenario). At a complex interchange with lane drops and merging traffic, stimuli are unexpected and the information load is high. PRT can increase by 35% or more in such conditions.

AASHTO provides decision sight distance (DSD) for these situations, with longer PRT assumptions. At 80 km/h, DSD for a speed/path change is about 267 m—more than double the SSD of ~128 m.

Using 2.5 s throughout would underestimate the required sight distance at the interchange and create a safety hazard. The engineer should apply DSD at the interchange section and transition to SSD where conditions are straightforward. SSD is the minimum for every road; DSD should be provided wherever drivers face unexpected or complex decisions.
